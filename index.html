Before Shelves Break

Dissonant Mind, Reverent Hall

A hymn marches softly through a chapel, the notes weaving around polished wood and floral pews, and I, in my seat, feel the tug of two truths that refuse to reconcile. I am taught love, the pure love of God, an acceptance and equality that enfolds all of humanity, yet… All? Are you sure?
I am reminded too of the lines drawn in holy text, in temple ordinances, that once said black hands could not hold power, that still say sisters in faith only have power in shadow, behind closed doors, lowered veils, and under males. Are they loved? Are you?
Hagar and Sariah, Leah and Rachel, the voices of women whisper across millennia: “I am bound, yet called blessed, to carry children for a sister who will always love me less. Promised blessings and protection, yet cast aside.” Is that what love is? Is that where it hides?
In a place sworn to truth but built with some lies. An affair or a command, an angel’s sword or just lust. Mistakes mixed with doctrine muddy the water. Hate and confusion stain the halls and history. The prophet as the man, or the mouthpiece? But isn’t love trust? How can I trust a Janus?
I dream of a justice that would allow my hands to act, but I hear a woman’s voice is soft in the halls of governance, her place beneath, supporting, auxiliary, never even a link in the chain. An exaltation whose asterisk is always *with your husband. Yet where am I in the promises he made? Words imply he is a King whether or not I stay.
I still kneel, still pray, my faith a rope that stretches between devotion and dissent, between hope and quiet despair. It is a strange devotion, a soft rebellion, to believe in equality while inhabiting inequity, to cherish commandments while questioning the lineage of laws, to love a church, a gospel, and grieve its silences.
I wonder at the God who commanded polygamy, who catalyzed rivalry between sisters, who allowed false tales of cursed skin and failed valiance on the tongues of His prophets, who declared faith intertwined with hierarchy, who hides a Mother, I long to meet. Is that equality? Is that all that is meant for me?
I feel the fire in myself and dare to hope it comes not with brimstone, but with aloe. Questions too deep to be mistaken as shallow. Holding on to the softness of the testimony of a child, but I fear. Is she strong enough to hold the weight my shelf bears? She must be for now, for what else can I do? We must keep trusting that the love is true.
So I live crushed in the weight of this contradiction, as Atlas of old, the truths in my hands, dense and dripping with history. I pray for wisdom, for courage, for clarity, for the day the stories of women and men, of all races, of all callings, will resonate as equal echoes of divinity. Until that day, as change drips slowly like wet sand in tapered glass, I will wait in patience, for Love to answer the questions I ask.

— Kimberly Williams

Feeling torn between two dissonant ideas can bring on feelings of anxiety, guilt, shame, stress, anger, confusion, and lonliness. Social pressures to "pick a side" only adds to the negative emotions. Cognitive dissonance can leave you feeling shattered and stuck between two impossible sides. Is it possible to make something beautiful from the pain?
